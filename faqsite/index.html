Screensaver of the Day FAQs

The Screensaver of the Day project aims to archive and share screensavers with people online who find themselves nostalgic for the times of their heyday.

All screensavers, well-known or obscure, old or new, basic or advanced, will be considered for this project. There are so many more screensavers out there than people know or remember. This project ideally works to share them all.

  • Why did you start this project?

    I started this project because I have a distinct nostalgia for these little programs. If you're of a certain age, you may not have paid too much attention to screensavers, but they were always there.

    Needing to be discreet by nature, they often blended into the background, and I find something about that fascinating. They were never meant to be noticed for more than a few seconds, and yet a wide variety were created and enjoyed by so many people.

    As a kid, I found myself entranced by them, especially ones that came in predictable, repeatable patterns. Through various old school tech accounts, I've discovered that I'm not the only one who fondly remembers screensavers, but there was no space dedicated to just them.

    I wanted to create that space and share them with all of you, especially given how uncommon they are in modern times. Join me in taking a trip back to simpler times!

  • What is a screensaver?

    Taken from Wikipedia: "A screensaver (or screen saver) is software that controls a monitor of the host computer with the intent of preventing screen burn-in for a screen susceptible to it. Generally, a screensaver starts controlling a monitor when the computer has been idle for a designated period of time and fills the screen either with black (all pixels off) or with changing graphics that tend to prevent each pixel from being on for a long time."

    They can also serve entertainment or security purposes in addition to their intended application.

    The following (in this context) are not screensavers:

    • A computer wallpaper or a phone background/lock screen
    • A sheet of plastic used to protect a screen from scratches

  • Is it screensaver or screen saver?

    Either spelling is accepted!

    John Socha, in his December 1983 Softalk for IBM article refers to his original program simply as Scrnsave.

    Personally, I use screensaver because it feels like more of a specific term for these programs. You sometimes see screen saver refer to things like cell phone screen protectors.

  • Can I contribute/suggest new screensavers?

    While I appreciate the enthusiasm, I am not currently accepting any new screensavers.

    I still have many in my database that I've yet to record, and I want to get through all of those before adding any new ones.

    I could just as easily download any archived screensaver I come across, but I personally take joy in having a physical collection, at least as a starting point.

    Perhaps at some point in the future, if the account grows large enough, I would consider changing that policy.

  • Where can I get screensavers for myself?

    Most desktop computers, modern TVs, and smartphones/tablets come with some sort of screen saving function. Most often, this consists of just turning off the screen after a certain amount of time, but I'm sure if you did a little research, you could find a way to set an actual screensaver for your devices.

    Many classic screensavers, such as some of the ones featured on this account, can easily be found on sites like archive.org if all you're interested in is downloading them. If you're more interested in the physical versions, eBay is a great place to find them. Keep in mind when looking for old screensavers that most only work on older 9x versions of Windows.

    Other sites such as Screensavers Planet aggregate many screensavers, but like similar aggrigator sites such as Softonic, I wouldn't trust these for a number of reasons.

    For some more modern one-off screensavers, you just have to know where to look. Developers will sometimes randomly release one as a one-off to little fanfare as an Easter Egg.

  • Which screensavers do you recommend?

    The easiest ones to set up and the ones that are most likely to work properly are generally the ones that come preincluded in the system. If you're new to everything or are a light computer user, I'd suggest starting here.

    If you have an Android, iOS, macOS, or Unix operating system, I would highly recommend checking out programming legend Jamie Zawinski's excellent 30+ year passion project, XScreenSaver. It comes with 200+ screensavers completely free!

    For classic screensavers, the After Dark releases are unmatched. Microsoft Plus! also provides great variety. With how big screensavers were in the 1990s, you'd be surprised just how many niche topics and interests had screensavers made for them, so exploring is encouraged!
